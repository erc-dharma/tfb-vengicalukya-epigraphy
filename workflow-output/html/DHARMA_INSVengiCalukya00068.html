Candavolu plates of Vijayāditya II or III

Hands:

Halantas. Final N in line 21 is essentially identical to a na, raised above normal height.

Original punctuation marks.

Other palaeographic observations. Anusvāra is above the character to which it belongs, or above headline to the right of that character; occasionally atop the next character, e.g. l13 kuṭuṁbinaḥ within the i marker. Short and long i/ī are not clearly distinguishable, but i seems to be a closed circle while ī appears to be open on the left, with a little hook at the opening; e.g. sti and śrī side by side in line 1; śī in line 5 is good example of the long ī. Headmarks are large horizontal lines with small horns on each side.

4 caḷ(u)kyānāṁ • The vowel of ḷ may be ū, or the engraver may have started engraving ḷya and then corrected.
10 (ṇaggiḥ vā)rā(tībhitī)-(kr̥ta) • There is little damage here; in fact the most damaged part of this locus is the last three characters, of whose reading I am quite certain. The preceding characters, however, are ambiguous on account of the script and the execution, and I am unable to offer a satisfactory reading. It is likely that multiple scribal errors are involved in addition to the ambiguous characters. The first problematic character, read as ṇa, might well be nā or vā, and reading ya or śā is not out of the question. The second definitely looks like ggi, but either of its consonant components may perhaps be dh instead of g. This is probably followed by a visarga, but it is just possible that this is instead a vowel marker (for e or part of o) attached to the next character. The third character looks much, but not entirely, like the first, so all possibilities listed for that also apply here, with the added complication that there may be a vowel marker prefixed to this character. This is definitely followed by rā, and the next two characters resemble one another, but are not identical. I read the consonant of the first as t, because this is the only way I can account for what looks like a notch in the upper outline of its body, which I take to be the hook of t. However, its shape in general looks more like bh, ś or n, less probably v or c. The second of the two similar characters is most likely bhī, but śī and nī are also possible. Any of the i-s in this stretch may be either short or long; I read long ī where the loop is open on the left, but this may be random variation. The closest to an intelligible phrase that I can obtain from the text is vāri-rāśi-śītīkr̥ta (assuming scribal omission of ri), but I have no inkling what ṇaggiḥ may be. I would expect this phrase to resemble the preceding and following qualifier, and thus to include a simile with iva and a double entendre. The double entendre may perhaps be that Vijayāditya’s army (cakra) is “cooled” (i.e., its martial ardour is diminished) only when it reaches the ocean; in this, he may be compared to the moon (?), whose orbit (cakra) is cooled by water and by the signs of the zodiac - but this is probably just my forced attempt to make sense of the text. There is also a possibility that the end of this locus should be read śitīkr̥ta (perhaps with a play on “sharpened discus” and “brightened domain”); or that ivārāti (perhaps with wordplay involving “enemy” and “not favour”) is to be read in the middle of this chunk. Related grants offer no better clue; the only comparable expressions involving cakra that I am aware of are cakradhara Ivāpratihata-cakraḥ in the Peṇukapaṟu grant of Jayasiṁha I and viṣṇor iva sva-cakra-nandakasya in the Koṟṟapaṟṟu grant of Vijayāditya II.
15 śarvvaśarmmaṇa(ḥ) • The ARIE report gives the father’s name as Bandhuśarman. While the initial śa is not unambiguously drawn, ndhu can be safely excluded. Compare rvva in line 13 and especially line 18.
17 magā(vaḍ)u • The ARIE report records the name of the village as Maṅgaveḍu. There is definitely no ṅ here, though an anusvāra may be present. The vertical vowel marker between g and v in my opinion bends to the left at the top, and is thus probably associated with g rather than with the next character.

Plates

1-14Greetings. The dear son of His Majesty King (mahārāja) Viṣṇuvardhana (IV or V)↓1 who attained victory in the clash of many a battle and who was eager to adorn the lineage of the majestic Caḷukyas—who are of the Mānavya gotra which is praised by the entire world, who are sons of Harīti, who attained kingship by the grace of Kauśikī’s boon, who are protected by the band of Mothers, who were deliberately appointed (to kingship) by Lord Mahāsena, to whom the realms of adversaries instantaneously submit at the [mere] sight of the superior Boar emblem they have acquired by the grace of the divine Nārāyaṇa, and whose bodies have been hallowed through washing in the purificatory ablutions (avabhr̥tha) of the Aśvamedha sacrifice—; grandson of King (mahārāja) Vijayāditya (I or II); [namely] the Supreme Lord (parameśvara) of Emperors (mahārājādhirāja) and Supreme Sovereign (parama-bhaṭṭāraka) Vijayāditya (II or III)—who desires the exaltation of his allies as Mount Meru {which indicates the rising of the sun}; whose circle (domain) is made ... ↓2; and who gives rise to prosperity as the ocean {produced Lakṣmī}—commands all householders (kuṭumbin)—including foremost the overseers (kūṭaka)— who reside in Gudravara district (viṣaya) as follows:

14-18Let it be known to you that to Yajñaśarman of the Bhāradvāja gotra and the Āpastambha sūtra—a resident of Krovaśiri who has studied the Veda, and who is the grandson of Kuppaśarman, a principal of the twiceborn,↓3 and son of Śarvaśarman—we have given a field of an extent (sufficient) for sowing ten kaṇḍikas of paddy seed at the village named Magāvaḍu.

18-21Its boundaries [are as follows]. To the east, the Kappa[?1×]ni field (cenu). To the south, the Ceṟukani field (cenu). To the west, the Godu[?1×]na Boḍu field (cenu). To the north, the Rājāḍi field (cenu). It is situated in the midst of these [boundaries].

Over and over again, Rāmabhadra begs all future kings thus: “Each in your own time, you shall respect this framework of legality that is universally applicable to kings!”

Notes

↑1. I do not know of any other Eastern Cālukya grant that mentions the reigning king’s father before his grandfather. It seems likely that starting out with Viṣṇuvardhana is a mistake in the composition, which was corrected by adding Vijayāditya afterward. This may have been simple oversight, or perhaps the text of the charter was based on a grant of the king’s father (Viṣṇuvardhana IV or V), which would have mentioned the grandfather (Viṣṇuvardhana III or IV) first. The composer of the present charter may have mechanically reproduced Viṣṇuvardhana here, and then changed the wording to make him the father of the present king.
↑2. See the apparatus to line 10 for some thoughts on the phrase that I am unable to interpret here.
↑3. The composer’s intent may have been to describe the donee, rather than the grandfather, as a principal of the twiceborn.

Commentary

The ARIE report reads the name of the donated village as Maṁgaveḍu, and identifies it with modern Maṅginapūḍi in Bandar taluk of Kistna district. The reading seems doubtful to me, and I therefore prefer to name the charter after its findspot. The report identifies Krovaśiri with modern Krōsūru in Sattenapalle taluk of Guntur district.

The attribution of this grant is questionable. ARIE talks about “early angular characters” in ascribing the plates to Vijayāditya II. Then, without skipping a breath, it states that the ājñapti of the grant is Paṇḍaraṅga, “the bee at the lotus-feet of Guṇagāṅka-Vijayāditya.” Neither the name nor the description appear anywhere in the photos available online.(The photos include one blank face, which is probably 1 recto, so it is possible that a description of the executor (perhaps an anuṣṭubh stanza) was added on 3 verso, of which no photo has been published.) The next item discussed in ARIE is a grant of Vijayāditya III (not published, said to include a complete genealogy with durations of reigns). There is a slight chance that the ARIE editors mixed up, and Paṇḍaraṅga is in fact mentioned in that grant. But if the ARIE is correct and Paṇḍaraṅga is mentioned in a part of the present grant that has not been photographed, then it must definitely be dated to the reign of Vijayāditya III. So long as this evidence is not available, I believe the present grant is slightly more likely to belong to Vijayāditya II. My reasons for believing this, none of them conclusive, are set out below.

Palaeographically, the script resembles the Koṟṟapaṟṟu grant of Vijayāditya II (which is neater, but has the same general ductus and same kind of horned headmarks), but is also similar to the Ciṁbuluru plates of Vijayāditya III.(As well as to the Ceruvu Mādhavaram plates of Kali Viṣṇuvardhana V (very similar, including the headmarks and ā and e markers; neater and more slanted), and to the Mḻopaṟṟu grant probably of Maṅgi Yuvarāja.) Likewise, it differs quite markedly from the Varppoṁgu plates of Vijayāditya II (whose characters are more rounded, with e.g. longer ra and ka, simpler ja and simple line headmarks), but also from the Ṭraṇḍapaṟu grant of Vijayāditya III (whose script is rounded in a more cursive and notchy way; the headmarks are shaped like a wide V or U).(As well as from the Pr̥thivipallavapaṭṭana grant of Viṣṇuvardhana IV or V.)

As for the contents, the two grants of Vijayāditya II known to me both record two ancestral generations, as does one of the two grants of Vijayāditya III whose text I have. The other known grant of Vijayāditya III gives the full king list with lengths of reigns, as does the charter of Vijayāditya III reported in ARIE after the present grant and most charters of subsequent rulers. The present grant records nothing about the reigning king’s grandfather and describes his father Viṣṇuvardhana as Aneka-samara-saṁghaṭṭaṇa-labdha-vijayasya. Similar phrases occur in several grants of the earlier rulers of the Eastern Cālukya family, but the last that I know of is in the Koṟṟapaṟṟu grant of Vijayāditya II, where that king’s grandfather is described as aneka-samara-saṁghaṭṭa-labdha-nija-bhuja-vijaya-śriyaḥ.

Of the phrases it uses to describe the reigning king, merur iva śaṁsita-mitrodayaḥ resembles pūrvvācalendra Iva mitrodayānukūla-mahimaḥ, used for Indra Bhaṭṭāraka in his sole known grant (Koṇḍaṇagūru), but has no other parallels I am aware of. The uninterpretable second phrase appears to be unique (see the apparatus to line 10). The last phrase, jalanidhir iva lakṣmī-prabhavaḥ, is paralleled by kṣīra-sāgara Iva lakṣmī-prabhavo, said of Vijayāditya I in his Eḍeru plates.

The descriptive phrases thus offer little help in attributing the present grant to one Vijayāditya or another, but tend to imply an earlier rather than a later king. All known grants of both the relevant Vijayādityas do include at least some description of the current king’s predecessors, but these descriptions are simpler (and thus more like the present one) in the two grants of Vijayāditya II than in the one grant of Vijayāditya III that does not record a complete king list. The brevity of the grant may thus in itself be an indication of relative earliness.

Several donees hailing from Krovaśiri are mentioned in the Koṟṟapaṟṟu grant of Vijayāditya II. Though they seem to have no other connection to the present donee, as none of them are of the Bhāradvāja gotra, this is nonetheless another tenuous link to Vijayāditya II.
