7 -di¡g!-maṇḍalasya • In gm, the g is formed as a downward curve of the left arm of a regularly placed ma. The likely reason is that this is the last line of the plate, right on the bottom edge, where a regularly composed ligature would not have fit. But correction from an initially engraved ma is also possible.
9 -vandita⟨ḥ⟩ ¡pā!⟨prā⟩ / ¡ṭaka!⟨rthaka⟩- • Hultzsch emends/standardises the same reading to -vandi-pāṭhaka-, which is perfectly plausible. I believe my suggestion is slightly more likely even though it involves a larger alteration to pāṭaka. Earlier Eastern Cālukya kings described as kāmadhenu direct their generosity at the needy and Brahmins (Cīpurupalle plates, line 5); supplicants (Timmapuram plates, line 9); and seekers of protection (Peṇukapaṟu grant, line 7; probably also Koṇeki grant, line 9).
10 ni⟨r⟩¡macchājy!⟨mātsary⟩(ā) ... ¡riṣya!⟨riṣṭa⟩- • Hultzsch emends/standardises the same reading to mātsaryālasya-naiṣṭhuryāriṣṭa-, which is again plausible. But again, I find it unlikely that ni was engraved superfluously (Hultzsch segments his text to read kāmadhenuni, which he emends to kāmadhenur) and prefer to assume that this syllable is part of the intended text. If so, then the compound ending with rahitaḥ must be separate from the one beginning with nir. The point of separation then must be where shown in my standardisation, as the first three words are definitely undesirable internal qualities, while the latter two (regardless of whether one construes riṣṭa or ariṣṭa) may be understood as malevolent external influences (though admittedly, paiśunya could also be an internal quality).

20 -¡prithivipo(ś)ī!- ◇ -p¡ri!⟨r̥⟩thiv¿i?⟨ī⟩pothī- EHHultzsch 1925-1926 • Hultzsch remarks that the character he reads as th is not quite the expected shape, but only goes on to state that it cannot be ṟ, as read in ARIE. He adds in his discussion that the second member of the name may be a misspelling of potī, the feminine of pota, which is known to have formed part of the names of Pallava kings. It may be possible to exclude my reading on the basis of a clearer facsimile, but in my scanned estampage, the character definitely appears to be ś. It has no headmark, but this is also true of several other instances of ś in the plates. If Sanskrit names are to be sought, pośī may be a non-standard spelling of poṣī (for poṣiṇī).

Commentary

The publication of these plates includes no image of the seal, so I reproduce its text as reported by Hultzsch in the text of his article, without indication of any unclarity.

Hultzsch identifies Maghiṁduvarāja (line 20) as the Pallava king Mahendravarman III and assumes that his daughter was a queen of Viṣṇuvardhana III. It seems to me far more likely that Maghiṁduvarāja (probably with the anusvāra to be read with ma) is Maṅgi Yuvarāja, and thus his daughter is a sister of Viṣṇuvardhana III. This is also the emendation made in Estienne-Monod’s etext, although her thesis (27) does not mention this, only repeats Hultzsch’s identification of this person. Compare also the spelling magiṁyuvarāja in line 11 of the Śrīpūṇḍi grant of Tāḻa II, with the anusvāra to the right of the vowel marker in giṁ. Why Hultzsch does not advocate this view, or even mention it as a possibility, is a mystery, especially in view of the fact that it is he who, in the same volume of Epigraphia Indica (258) asserts that duvarāja is a Dravidian tadbhava form of yuvarāja, and that the Pr̥thividuvarāja mentioned in the Kopparam plates of Pulakeśin II must be Pr̥thivīyuvarāja, i.e. Viṣṇuvardhana I. Sircar (in 254) asserts the same view and dismisses identification with Mahendravarman III.
