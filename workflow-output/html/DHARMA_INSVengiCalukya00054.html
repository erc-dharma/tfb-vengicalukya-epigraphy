Editors:

Identifier:

INSVengiCalukya00054

Hands:

Halantas. Final T in l1 seems to be a slightly reduced ta with a headmark much like the regular one; it may also be an erroneous ta instead of T.

Original punctuation marks

Other palaeographic observations. The consonants ka and ra occur in both short and long forms. Anusvāra is usually above the character to which it belongs, but often to the right of that consonant and occasionally atop the next character (e.g. l8 maṁgi; l9 kuṭiṁbinas; l11 vedāṁga; l22, ṁjalir). Dependent i and ī are rarely distinguished and are read as expected where there is no clear indication of either. See also the commentary.

Translation by Dániel Balogh

1-9Greetings. From the ocean that is the lineage of the Caḷukyas—who were deliberately appointed (to kingship) by the Divine Lord Mahāsena, who are protected by the band of Mothers, who are of the Mānavya gotra, who are sons of Hāritī, who attained kingship by the grace of Kauśikī’s boon, who perform the Aśvamedha sacrifice, and whose fame has spread as far as the oceans—had arisen a moon [who was] Kīrtivarman, who earned his great reputation by his political acumen (naya), discipline (vinaya) and valour. His son [was] His Majesty King (mahārāja) Viṣṇuvardhana, who perfumed the complete circle of the quarters with pleasant fragrance from the efflorescence, which is glory, sprouting from the liana that is the victory goddess attained in the clash of many a battle. His son, His Majesty the supremely pious Junior Vallabha King (yuva-vallabha-mahārāja) Maṅgi, whose pair of feet are tinted by the hues of the rays from the gems fitted to the surfaces of the crowns of all the enemy kings bowed down by fear of the power of his arms that were a shelter to the complete world (sakala-lokāśraya) illuminated by his valour [unique] in the three worlds, commands all householders (kuṭumbin)—including foremost the territorial overseers (rāṣṭrakūṭa)—¿in Canuḻpalli? district (viṣaya) as follows.

9-13Let it be known to you [that] on the occasion of the winter solstice we have given the village named ¿Mḻopaṟṟu? with exemption from all taxes, [the donation being] sanctified by (a libation of) water, to Maṇḍaśarman, who is thoroughly versed in the Vedas and Vedāṅgas and devoted to the six duties, ¿and who is the grandson of Agniśarman, a hiraṇyakeśin of the Vatsa gotra residing in Vanapaṟṟu and the son of [3×] ¿Āḍ?iśarman?↓1—to [this] Maṇḍaśarman ¿who is? devoted to ¿his? lord Maṅgi Yuvarāja ¿in ... (astivihasana) Maṇḍūṟūra?.↓2

13-16Its boundaries [are as follows]. To the east, ¿Velḻa?vaṟṟu. To the south, Pulgoṭlapabulūru. To the west, ¿Agūnakṣayyaṁ?. ↓3 To the north, ... (¿kḻocce ḍoḍuva?). [The donated land is] situated in between these. Let no-one pose an obstacle (to the enjoyment of rights) over it. He who does so shall be conjoined with the five great sins. Vyāsa too has said:

I.
Many (kings) have granted land, and many have preserved it (as formerly granted). Whosoever at any time owns the land, the fruit {reward (accrued of granting it)} belongs to him at that time.

II.
He who would seize land, whether given by himself or by another, shall be born as a worm in faeces for sixty thousand years.

III.
A seizer of (granted) land cannot be purified [even] by donating ten million cows, nor by [performing] a hundred Aśvamedhas, [nor by constructing] a thousand tanks.

IV.
It is not [actual] poison that is [properly] called poison: it is the property of a Brahmin that is said to be poison. Poison kills just the one man, while [seizing] the property of a Brahmin [destroys] his progeny.

V.
Hereby I offer my respectful obeisance (añjali) to [all] future kings on earth, born in my lineage and in different royal lineages, who with minds averted from sin observe this ruling (dharma) of mine in its integrity.

VI.
O king, those donations given by kings in the past to generate merit (dharma), wealth (artha) and fame, are [now] comparable to discarded garlands or vomit. What decent man would ever partake again of them?

VII.
Over and over again, Rāmadeva↓4 begs all these future kings: “Each in your own time, you shall respect this framework of legality that is universally applicable to kings!”

VIII.
The executor (ājñapti) in this [matter] is the lord (īśa) Ghanarāśi.↓5 His Honour Bhānuśakti, beneficient to subjects

26[is] the ¿writer? of this decree↓6

Notes

↑1. According to RM’s commentary, there are three donees: Agniśarman, his grandson Suśarman (read by RM where I show a lacuna) and Maṇḍaśarman (son of Veṁgiśarman, the name I tentatively read as Āḍiśarman). I am, however, quite certain in spite of the necessity of a minor emendation, that the donee is Maṇḍaśarman, son of Āḍiśarman and grandson of Agniśarman. By RM’s reading, only Agniśarman would have a residence, gotra and school, but he would have no named ancestors; the next person (the ostensible Suśarman) would have only a named grandfather, while Maṇḍaśarman would only have a named father. It is much more logical to assume a single Brahmanical lineage of three generations, with residence, gotra and school recorded only for the grandfather, as commonly done in related grants.
↑2. I cannot interpret this phrase; see the apparatus to line 11.
↑3. Could akṣayya mean a previously granted Brahmanical holding (akṣaya-nīvī)?
↑4. The name in this stanza is normally Rāmabhadra.
↑5. Or, given the ending of his name rāśi, perhaps īśa should be understood to mean that Ghanarāśi is Śiva, i.e. a fully initiated Śaiva.
↑6. The text ends abruptly here. See the commentary.

Commentary

The text ends abruptly with the last line on 3r. I agree with RM that only a very small amount of text seems to be missing here, most probably identifying the writer of the text. RM believes a date was also lost here, but I do not find this likely on the basis of parallel grants. It is not clear whether the last bit of text in line 26 is prose or a garbled continuation of the indravajrā hemistich identifying the executor; nor is it clear whether Ghanarāśi and Bhānuśakti are two separate persons or only one. The intended text may for instance have been something like tac-chāsanasyāsya ca lekha-karttā. The photographs published by the National Museum include a picture of 3v, and there seems to be no trace of writing there whatsoever. Perhaps the text was intended to be complete as it is. The last character, li, may have been intended as an abbreviation for likhita, and the symbol after it might be an abbreviation marker. Assuming that the second hemistich intended by the composer was shortened by the scribe to fit the text on the plate, it seems most likely that Ghanarāśi was the executor and Bhānuśakti was the writer.

The issuer of these plates was identified in the ARIE report as Jayasiṁha I on the grounds that the seal bears the legend sarvasiddhi and only Kīrtivarman and Viṣṇuvardhana (I) are mentioned as ancestors in the genealogy. RM, however, asserts that maṅgi-yuva-vallabha-mahārāja is clearly the donor. While I must point out that neither instance of the name maṁgi is quite clear, the reading is indeed quite secure. RM adds that, since Maṅgi Yuvarāja’s seals have the legend vijayasiddhi, the seal may not be the original seal of this grant. He further supposes that reference to the closer ancestry of Maṅgi Yuvarāja was apparently ‘left out by oversight’. His editor in EI (probably K. G. Krishnan) adds in a footnote that there may have been ‘a mix up of two different plates at a distant time’. Moreover, RM notes that the donor’s epithet is sakala-lokāśraya in line 6, but says only that this ‘means the same’ as Maṅgi Yuvarāja’s regular epithet sarvalokāśraya. However, the two epithets are not necessarily interchangeable. The question of the issuer needs further study.

Some details to consider are the following.

  • 1, The phraseology of the grant has much in common with known grants of Maṅgi Yuvarāja. However, it has just as much in common with grants of Jayasiṁha I and Viṣṇuvardhana II, most especially with the Koṇeki grant of Viṣṇuvardhana II. Particularly noteworthy is the fact that all grants of Maṅgi known to me begin with a more mature version of the stock text śrīmatāṁ sakala-.
  • 2, The genealogy begins with Kīrtivarman, whereas grants of Maṅgi known to me begin either with Jayasiṁha I or with Kubja Viṣṇuvardhana. Genealogies beginning with Kīrtivarman are found in all grants of Jayasiṁha I that I know, one grant of Viṣṇuvardhana II (of the two I know), and the single known grant of Indra Bhaṭṭāraka.
  • 3, The exhortatory verses are unusual. Stanza 3 does not occur in any Eastern Cālukya grant I know of. Variants of stanza 4 occur (sporadically) only from the time of Vijayāditya I onward, and the first closely related variant is in a grant of Kali Viṣṇuvardhana. Stanza 6 occurs in at least one grant of Pulakeśin II, but among Eastern Cālukya charters, its only occurrence that I know of is in one of Bhīma I.
  • 4, I have not verified this, but the elaborate headmarks of this inscription (usually long horizontal lines with the ends curved up) seem late to me. It may be that a later scribe was attempting to imitate an earlier style of writing. A more detailed study of the palaeography is warranted.

The idea that plates belonging to different grants have been mixed up strikes me as preposterous, since the page breaks concerned would have had to be at the same spot in the text. The idea that Indra Bhaṭṭāraka and Viṣṇuvardhana II were omitted out of oversight is likewise preposterous; moreover, the text progresses smoothly through kīrttivarmmaṇaḥ sūnor … śrī-viṣṇuvarddhana-mahārājasya priya-tanayaḥ … śrī-maṁgi(?)-yuva-vallabha-mahārājaḥ. Any omitted people would have had to have been included in the draft before priya-tanayaḥ, and the syntax would have been quite awkward. Forgery (produced some generations after Maṅgi Yuvarāja and probably using a genuine seal) seems quite likely, but I would expect a forger to have copied a specimen grant with alteration only in the executive section, rather than cobbling the text together from grants of different rulers.

The possibility also remains that it is an idiosyncratic charter of the Maṅgi Yuvarāja we are familiar with. And there is, in addition, some chance that we are dealing here with an unknown Maṅgi Yuvarāja from an earlier time. This person could be either Jayasiṁha I before his formal accession to the throne, or Indra Bhaṭṭāraka. The former did use the epithets sakala-lokāśraya and (probably) trailokya-vikrama, and if the problematic locus in line 8 correctly reads śrīmachrī instead of śrīmaṁgi, then “the majestic yuvarāja, son of Viṣṇuvardhana” would clearly identify him. The latter, in turn, may well have spent part (perhaps a large part) of his life as yuvarāja under Jayasiṁha I. Although he is not known to have been called sakala-lokāśraya (indeed, he was, at least after his coronation, siṁha-vikrama and, probably, rāja-lokāśraya), yet if his personal name had been Maṅgi and if yuvarāja had stuck to him as a permanent epithet, then that would to some degree explain why his grandson bore the name Maṅgi Yuvarāja as a crowned king.
