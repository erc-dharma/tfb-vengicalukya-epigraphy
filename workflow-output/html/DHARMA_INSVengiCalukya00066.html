Other palaeographic observations. Anusvāra is normally to the right of the character to which it belongs, but may be above it; it is possibly inside the i marker on the next character in l15 vaṁgipaṟu. Retroflex ṇa has a shape that I find unusual, with the left wing bent down to form a closed loop with the top of the body. Doubling of consonants occasionally omitted after r, e.g. l8 suvarṇa, l19 sarva, pūrvataḥ. Possible occasional doubling before r: sagotrāṇāṁ in l1-l2 may be spelt with ttr (which is how SR prints it), but this is not clear enough and the tr of putrāṇāṁ shortly afterward is definitely single.

9 mahā{ḥ}rājasya ◇ mahārājasya SRSubba Rao 1930-1931 — 9 (s)⟨v⟩(a-pratā)pāva(nata-) ◇ sapratāpāvanata- SRSubba Rao 1930-1931 • The writing is badly worn here and it is not impossible that v is in fact present. I prefer to read/restore sva in preference to sa. Related grants use sva-bhuja-bala-pratāpāvanata or simply pratāpāvanata in similar contexts, while the Varppoṁgu plates of Vijayāditya II read pratāpānurāgāvanata-samasta-sāmanta-cakkraḥ in an almost identical context.

13 (nemma)ḷūru ◇ gommaḷūru SRSubba Rao 1930-1931 • ARIE reports the name of the village as Nemmalūru. The first character is indistinct in the estampage, but SR’s go appears to be impossible from the vestiges (compare go in lines 1 and 15). What is visible in the estampage consists of a horizontal line with a downward hook at the left, which is likely an e marker; a curved stem attaching this stroke to the body on the right-hand edge of the character; and the upper left portion of the body, which resembles a quarter oval. The shape is most reminiscent of a partially preserved kha, but khā in line 14 is too damaged to serve for a comparison. Indications for this and against ne are the curly left end of the upper horizontal stroke (which would probably be less curly if it were an e) and by the curvature of the vertical stroke (where a straight vertical stem would be expected in n). These indications are not, however, decisive. I assume that the ARIE editor had a good look at the original plates and was therefore in a better position to judge. Moreover, there is a present-day village Nimmaluru at 16° 16′ 13.08″ N, 80° 59′ 48.12″ E, in Gudivada taluk of Krishna district (so probably in Gudrahāra Viṣaya), though not in West Godavari district, where the inscription is said to have been found. There is a stream (koḍu?) to the west of this village, but I find no names recognisable from the grant in its vicinity. For these considerations I prefer the reading nemmaḷūru, but khammaḷūru may also be correct, and other readings such as vemmaḷūru cannot be excluded.

19–20 ciḷḷeruguḷa maḍḍa/bu ◇ ciṭṭeruguṭa maṭṭa/bu SRSubba Rao 1930-1931 • — 19 (ce)ndaṟū-(bo)ya- ◇ ṣendaṟūbāya SRSubba Rao 1930-1931 • The latter half of this word is quite certainly boya. The first character is uncertain, but definitely not ṣe, which may be a typo in SR’s edition. In addition to ce, it may be co, kha or khā. The second character does not appear to be ṇḍa, but that may have been intended if the first character is kha.
21 pḻ(āndaṟo)bu ◇ ppāṇḍaṟabu SRSubba Rao 1930-1931 • Being unable to interpret this word, I am uncertain of its reading. The subscript component of the first character is definitely ḻ. Its vowel marker rises very high above the body before bending down and would imply the vowel au. There is definitely no second stroke on the left, but the au of pautrikaṁ in line 28 may also be written with a single cursive stroke. Nonetheless, au being unlikely in a vernacular word, the intent was probably ā; the vowel marker in ṣā a little further on in this line rises almost as high. The second character is probably nda, but ṇḍa, nde and ndo are also possible. The third character’s consonant component may be ḻ, but ṟ is more likely. There seems to be a small vowel marker attached to its body on the right, and there is plenty of space to its left, so I assume that there was a second vowel marker (together for o) here.

Moreover, Boḻama is also the executor of the Zulakallu and Eḍeru of plates Vijayāditya I. This has also been pointed out by G. S.
