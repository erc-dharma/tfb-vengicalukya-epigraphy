The emendation kaṭaka would be appropriate to the context, but it is no less unmetrical than kuṭku. The form kaḍa-rāja may have been used here, e.g. as Ājñaptir asyāḥ kaḍa-rāja. They may have understood samullayati to stand for samullikhita or a similar word. The text as read and emended by them is incorrect both metrically and semantically, so I cannot accept their interpretation. I do not find loka-netra likely to be a name and believe that the stanza is a coherent one that speaks only about the executor. Nonetheless, my own restoration is only tentative.

Plates

1-16↓1Greetings from majestic Piṣṭapura. ¿In the lineage of the majestic Cālukyas,? who were deliberately appointed (to kingship) by the Lord Mahāsena, who are the sons of Hāriti, who are of the Mānavya gotra, who have attained kingship ¿through the boon of Lord Kumāra?,↓2 who perform the Aśvamedha sacrifice, there arose Kīrtivarman for the salvation of living beings, as nectar (amr̥ta) [arose] in the great ocean (for the salvation of living beigns). His son, deliberately appointed (as successor) by him, was King (mahārāja) Viṣṇuvardhana, the majestic Crocodile-bannered (love god) (makaradhvaja) resembling another Viṣṇu, who ¿acquired? the Boar ¿emblem? by means of his three powers (śakti-traya), who was ¿likewise? recognised among the people for his just reputation, victorious in diverse battles, whose two lotus feet are ¿set with? rays from the gemstones on the crowns of kings. His son, deliberately appointed (as successor) by him, [namely] His Majesty King (mahārāja) Jayasiṁha, ... ↓3 who is courageous like a lion (siṁha), in order to increase his mother’s and father’s merit, has ¿given? the village named Viḷeṣṭi, [located] on the northern bank of the (river) Kr̥ṣṇa-beṇṇā in Nātavāḍi district (viṣaya) converted into a rent-free holding (agrahāra) by a remission of all burdens, to the ¿grandson? of the caturvedin Bhūtisvāmin, the son of Poḷasvāmin, [namely] the dvivedin Svāmiśarman of the Kautsa gotra, a ... ↓4 residing in Guntapura, [the donation being] sanctified by (a libation of) water.

16-18Let [this] be known to all residents of the district and to our (Jayasiṁha’s) officials (bhr̥tya). All ¿kings learned in? the legal treatises (dharma-śāstra) ¿must always? safeguard their own righteousness (sva-dharma).↓5 ¿In this connection, the Lord Brahmā too? has said:

I.
A donor of land rejoices in heaven for sixty millennia, [while] a seizer [of granted land] and a condoner [of such seizure] shall reside in hell for just as many.

II.
Many [kings] have granted land, and many have preserved [former grants]. Whosoever at any time owns the land, the fruit {reward} [accrued of granting it] belongs to him at that time.

III.
↓6A donor of land rejoices in heaven for sixty millennia, [while] a seizer [of granted land] and a condoner [of such seizure] shall reside in hell for just as many.

IV.
Many [kings] have granted land, and many have preserved [former grants]. Whosoever at any time owns the land, the fruit/reward [accrued of granting it] belongs to him at that time.

V.
O Yudhiṣṭhira, diligently preserve land that has been donated, whether by yourself or another. O best of land-possessors, preserving [a grant] is superior to making a grant.

VI.
What decent man would ever partake again of those donations ¿given by kings in the past? to generate merit (dharma), wealth (artha) and fame, which are [now] comparable to discarded garlands or vomit?↓7

VII.
The executor (ājñapti) of this (decree) is ¿the chamberlain Kaṇḍi?, a foundation of proper order (dharma), a wishing-tree for subjects, majestic [yet] also approachable by virtuous men, ¿who always brightens the eyes of the populace?.↓8

Notes

↑1. The text of these plates has been tentatively reconstructed on the basis of KPS’s substandard edition. The accuracy of the translation is consequently low. Translations of segments where I conjecturally ovverride the text reported by KPS are marked as uncertain, but there may well be errors in my translation of other passages as well.
↑2. I have strong doubts about the correctness of the reading of this phrase; see the apparatus to line 2
↑3. I am unable to reconstruct the text here. It may have said something about Jayasiṁha’s fame extending to the oceans. See the apparatus to line 9.
↑4. I cannot interpret the reported reading gonvāyine; see the apparatus to line 12. The word may describe either Svāmiśarman (if the case ending is correct) or his father Poḷasvāmin (on the basis of its position in the sentence).
↑5. I reconstruct this sentence tentatively; see the apparatus notes to line 17. If my reconstruction is largely correct, then the purport of the text is that future kings who might consider revoking this grant made by Jayasiṁha should refrain from doing so in order not to acquire demerit and a bad reputation.
↑6. Stanzas I and IV are repetitions of stanzas I and II, for no clear reason.
↑7. I translate the standard text of this stanza. The first hemistich may have been inscribed with errors, or with an unusual variant of this verse. See the apparatus to lines 25 and 26.
↑8. This stanza is tentatively reconstructed and translated; see the apparatus to lines 28 and 29.

Commentary

Very unusually, both sides of the first plate are inscribed.

Bibliography

Reported in ARIE 1990-1991: page 23, appendix A/1990–91, № 8 with some further details at ARIE 1990-1991: pages 3–4. Edited from inked impressions by Madhav N. Katti and C. A. Padmanabha Sastry (1995), without facsimiles and without translation. The present edition by Dániel Balogh is based on the published edition. However, this edition contains such an inordinate number of typographic (or other) mistakes, that duplicating it to the letter would serve no purpose, and in some cases the intent of the editors cannot be reconstructed. My edition therefore shows my conjectures of what the actual reading of the plates may be and how that should in my opinion be emended. Every locus where my “reading” of the assumed original diverges from the printed edition is marked up as unclear and tentatively in my edition, whereas loci marked as unclear by the original editors are shown here as unclear, but confidently read. My apparatus cites the printed edition where I deviate from it in a major way or where my conjecture is uncertain, but I silently correct some obvious typos, ignore some nonsensical emendations, and apply my own segmentation.
